Two-Population Proportion Tests – Worked Examples

Each problem below is a hypothesis test for the difference between two population proportions. The solutions follow the standard steps: (1) define parameters and hypotheses, (2) check conditions, (3) compute the pooled proportion, (4) find the standard error and test statistic, (5) compute the p-value, (6) interpret the result in context.

Example 1 – Influencer Click-Through Rates

Two social media influencers, A and B, are running sponsored posts for the same product. For a particular week:

  • Influencer A had 200 followers see the sponsored post, and 50 of them clicked the link.
  • Influencer B had 180 followers see the sponsored post, and 27 of them clicked the link.

We want to know if there is evidence that Influencer A has a higher true click-through rate than Influencer B. Use a significance level of \(\alpha = 0.05\).

Show full solution

Step 1. Define parameters and hypotheses

Let:

  • \(p_1\) = true click-through proportion for Influencer A.
  • \(p_2\) = true click-through proportion for Influencer B.

We are interested in whether \(p_1\) is higher than \(p_2\). The hypotheses:

\[ \begin{aligned} H_0 &: p_1 - p_2 = 0 \quad (\text{no difference in true click-through rates}),\\[4pt] H_a &: p_1 - p_2 > 0 \quad (\text{A has a higher true click-through rate than B}). \end{aligned} \]

Step 2. Sample proportions and conditions

From the data:

  • Influencer A: \(n_1 = 200\), \(x_1 = 50\), so \(\hat p_1 = \dfrac{50}{200} = 0.25.\)
  • Influencer B: \(n_2 = 180\), \(x_2 = 27\), so \(\hat p_2 = \dfrac{27}{180} = 0.15.\)

Check large counts:

\[ \begin{aligned} n_1\hat p_1 &= 200 \cdot 0.25 = 50 \ge 10, \\ n_1(1 - \hat p_1) &= 200 \cdot 0.75 = 150 \ge 10, \\ n_2\hat p_2 &= 180 \cdot 0.15 = 27 \ge 10, \\ n_2(1 - \hat p_2) &= 180 \cdot 0.85 = 153 \ge 10. \end{aligned} \]

All are at least 10, so the normal approximation is reasonable. We also assume the two groups of followers are independent.

Step 3. Pooled proportion under the null

Under \(H_0\), we treat both samples as coming from one population with a common true click-through rate \(p\). The pooled estimate is:

\[ \hat p = \frac{x_1 + x_2}{n_1 + n_2} = \frac{50 + 27}{200 + 180} = \frac{77}{380} \approx 0.2026. \]

Step 4. Standard error and test statistic

Standard error:

\[ SE = \sqrt{\,\hat p(1 - \hat p)\left(\frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2}\right)} = \sqrt{0.2026 \cdot 0.7974 \left(\frac{1}{200} + \frac{1}{180}\right)}. \]

Compute:

\[ \hat p(1 - \hat p) \approx 0.2026 \cdot 0.7974 \approx 0.1616, \] \[ \frac{1}{200} + \frac{1}{180} = 0.005 + 0.005555\ldots \approx 0.0106. \]

\[ SE \approx \sqrt{0.1616 \cdot 0.0106} \approx \sqrt{0.00171} \approx 0.0413. \]

The difference in sample proportions:

\[ \hat p_1 - \hat p_2 = 0.25 - 0.15 = 0.10. \]

Test statistic:

\[ z = \frac{(\hat p_1 - \hat p_2) - 0}{SE} = \frac{0.10}{0.0413} \approx 2.42. \]

Step 5. p-value and decision

This is a right-tailed test (\(H_a: p_1 - p_2 > 0\)), so:

\[ p\text{-value} = P(Z \ge 2.42). \]

From the standard normal distribution, this probability is about \(0.0077\) (less than 1%).

Since \(p\text{-value} \approx 0.0077 < \alpha = 0.05\), we reject \(H_0\).

Step 6. Conclusion in context

At the 5% significance level, there is strong evidence that Influencer A has a higher true click-through rate than Influencer B for this product, at least for this week and these audiences.

Example 2 – Teaching Method and Pass Rates

A college is comparing a traditional lecture-based teaching method with a new, more interactive method for an introductory course. At the end of the term:

  • In the traditional section, 30 of 50 students passed the final exam.
  • In the interactive section, 42 of 60 students passed the final exam.

Is there evidence that the interactive method has a different pass rate than the traditional method? Use \(\alpha = 0.05\) and a two-sided test.

Show full solution

Step 1. Parameters and hypotheses

Let:

  • \(p_1\) = true pass rate for the traditional method.
  • \(p_2\) = true pass rate for the interactive method.

We are testing for any difference (not specifically higher or lower), so:

\[ \begin{aligned} H_0 &: p_1 - p_2 = 0, \\ H_a &: p_1 - p_2 \neq 0. \end{aligned} \]

Step 2. Sample proportions and conditions

  • Traditional: \(n_1 = 50\), \(x_1 = 30\), \(\hat p_1 = \dfrac{30}{50} = 0.60.\)
  • Interactive: \(n_2 = 60\), \(x_2 = 42\), \(\hat p_2 = \dfrac{42}{60} = 0.70.\)

Counts:

\[ \begin{aligned} n_1\hat p_1 &= 50 \cdot 0.60 = 30 \ge 10, \\ n_1(1 - \hat p_1) &= 50 \cdot 0.40 = 20 \ge 10, \\ n_2\hat p_2 &= 60 \cdot 0.70 = 42 \ge 10, \\ n_2(1 - \hat p_2) &= 60 \cdot 0.30 = 18 \ge 10. \end{aligned} \]

All are large enough for the normal approximation.

Step 3. Pooled proportion

\[ \hat p = \frac{x_1 + x_2}{n_1 + n_2} = \frac{30 + 42}{50 + 60} = \frac{72}{110} \approx 0.6545. \]

Step 4. Standard error and \(z\)-statistic

\[ SE = \sqrt{\,\hat p(1 - \hat p)\left(\frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2}\right)} = \sqrt{0.6545 \cdot 0.3455 \left(\frac{1}{50} + \frac{1}{60}\right)}. \]

\[ \hat p(1 - \hat p) \approx 0.6545 \cdot 0.3455 \approx 0.226, \] \[ \frac{1}{50} + \frac{1}{60} = 0.02 + 0.0167 \approx 0.0367. \]

\[ SE \approx \sqrt{0.226 \cdot 0.0367} \approx \sqrt{0.00829} \approx 0.0911. \]

Difference in sample proportions:

\[ \hat p_1 - \hat p_2 = 0.60 - 0.70 = -0.10. \]

\[ z = \frac{(\hat p_1 - \hat p_2) - 0}{SE} = \frac{-0.10}{0.0911} \approx -1.10. \]

Step 5. p-value and decision

This is a two-sided test, so:

\[ p\text{-value} = 2P(Z \ge |z|) = 2P(Z \ge 1.10). \]

From the standard normal table, \(P(Z \ge 1.10)\) is about \(0.136\), so:

\[ p\text{-value} \approx 2 \cdot 0.136 = 0.272. \]

Since \(p\text{-value} \approx 0.272 > 0.05\), we fail to reject \(H_0\).

Step 6. Conclusion in context

At the 5% significance level, we do not have strong evidence that the true pass rate differs between the traditional and interactive teaching methods. The observed difference (60% vs 70%) could plausibly be due to random variation.

Example 3 – On-Time Assignment Submissions (Online vs In-Person)

An instructor teaches the same course in two formats: one online and one in-person. Over the term, the instructor tracks whether students submit a particular major assignment on time.

  • In the online section, 96 out of 150 students submitted the assignment on time.
  • In the in-person section, 105 out of 140 students submitted the assignment on time.

The instructor suspects that the online section might have a lower true on-time submission rate than the in-person section. Test this claim at the \(\alpha = 0.05\) level using a left-tailed test.

Show full solution

Step 1. Parameters and hypotheses

Let:

  • \(p_1\) = true on-time submission rate in the online section.
  • \(p_2\) = true on-time submission rate in the in-person section.

The suspicion is that \(p_1\) is less than \(p_2\). So:

\[ \begin{aligned} H_0 &: p_1 - p_2 = 0, \\ H_a &: p_1 - p_2 < 0 \quad (\text{online section has a lower true on-time rate}). \end{aligned} \]

Step 2. Sample proportions and conditions

  • Online: \(n_1 = 150\), \(x_1 = 96\), \(\hat p_1 = \dfrac{96}{150} = 0.64.\)
  • In-person: \(n_2 = 140\), \(x_2 = 105\), \(\hat p_2 = \dfrac{105}{140} = 0.75.\)

Counts:

\[ \begin{aligned} n_1\hat p_1 &= 150 \cdot 0.64 = 96 \ge 10, \\ n_1(1 - \hat p_1) &= 150 \cdot 0.36 = 54 \ge 10, \\ n_2\hat p_2 &= 140 \cdot 0.75 = 105 \ge 10, \\ n_2(1 - \hat p_2) &= 140 \cdot 0.25 = 35 \ge 10. \end{aligned} \]

Normal approximation conditions are satisfied. We also assume the two sections’ students are independent groups.

Step 3. Pooled proportion

\[ \hat p = \frac{x_1 + x_2}{n_1 + n_2} = \frac{96 + 105}{150 + 140} = \frac{201}{290} \approx 0.6931. \]

Step 4. Standard error and \(z\)-statistic

\[ SE = \sqrt{\,\hat p(1 - \hat p)\left(\frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2}\right)}. \]

\[ \hat p(1 - \hat p) \approx 0.6931 \cdot 0.3069 \approx 0.2128, \] \[ \frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2} = \frac{1}{150} + \frac{1}{140} \approx 0.00667 + 0.00714 \approx 0.01381. \]

\[ SE \approx \sqrt{0.2128 \cdot 0.01381} \approx \sqrt{0.00294} \approx 0.0542. \]

Difference in sample proportions:

\[ \hat p_1 - \hat p_2 = 0.64 - 0.75 = -0.11. \]

Test statistic:

\[ z = \frac{(\hat p_1 - \hat p_2) - 0}{SE} = \frac{-0.11}{0.0542} \approx -2.03. \]

Step 5. p-value and decision

This is a left-tailed test (\(H_a: p_1 - p_2 < 0\)), so:

\[ p\text{-value} = P(Z \le -2.03). \]

From the standard normal distribution, \(P(Z \le -2.03)\) is about \(0.021\).

Since \(p\text{-value} \approx 0.021 < 0.05\), we reject \(H_0\).

Step 6. Conclusion in context

At the 5% significance level, there is statistical evidence that the online section has a lower true on-time submission rate than the in-person section for this assignment.

Example 4 – Email Marketing Subject Lines

A company is A/B testing two email subject lines for a marketing campaign.

  • Subject Line A was sent to 300 customers; 84 of them opened the email.
  • Subject Line B was sent to 280 customers; 75 of them opened the email.

Does the data provide evidence that the true open rates for the two subject lines are different? Use a two-sided test with \(\alpha = 0.05\).

Show full solution

Step 1. Parameters and hypotheses

Let:

  • \(p_1\) = true proportion of customers who open emails with Subject Line A.
  • \(p_2\) = true proportion of customers who open emails with Subject Line B.

We are testing for any difference:

\[ \begin{aligned} H_0 &: p_1 - p_2 = 0, \\ H_a &: p_1 - p_2 \neq 0. \end{aligned} \]

Step 2. Sample proportions and conditions

  • Subject A: \(n_1 = 300\), \(x_1 = 84\), \(\hat p_1 = \dfrac{84}{300} = 0.28.\)
  • Subject B: \(n_2 = 280\), \(x_2 = 75\), \(\hat p_2 = \dfrac{75}{280} \approx 0.2679.\)

Counts:

\[ \begin{aligned} n_1\hat p_1 &= 300 \cdot 0.28 = 84 \ge 10, \\ n_1(1 - \hat p_1) &= 300 \cdot 0.72 = 216 \ge 10, \\ n_2\hat p_2 &\approx 280 \cdot 0.2679 \approx 75 \ge 10, \\ n_2(1 - \hat p_2) &\approx 280 \cdot 0.7321 \approx 205 \ge 10. \end{aligned} \]

Conditions for the normal approximation are satisfied.

Step 3. Pooled proportion

\[ \hat p = \frac{x_1 + x_2}{n_1 + n_2} = \frac{84 + 75}{300 + 280} = \frac{159}{580} \approx 0.2741. \]

Step 4. Standard error and \(z\)-statistic

\[ SE = \sqrt{\,\hat p(1 - \hat p)\left(\frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2}\right)} = \sqrt{0.2741 \cdot 0.7259 \left(\frac{1}{300} + \frac{1}{280}\right)}. \]

\[ \hat p(1 - \hat p) \approx 0.2741 \cdot 0.7259 \approx 0.199, \] \[ \frac{1}{300} + \frac{1}{280} \approx 0.00333 + 0.00357 = 0.00690. \]

\[ SE \approx \sqrt{0.199 \cdot 0.00690} \approx \sqrt{0.00137} \approx 0.0371. \]

Difference in sample proportions:

\[ \hat p_1 - \hat p_2 \approx 0.28 - 0.2679 \approx 0.0121. \]

Test statistic:

\[ z = \frac{(\hat p_1 - \hat p_2) - 0}{SE} \approx \frac{0.0121}{0.0371} \approx 0.33. \]

Step 5. p-value and decision

Two-sided test:

\[ p\text{-value} = 2P(Z \ge |0.33|). \]

\(P(Z \ge 0.33)\) is about \(0.372\). So:

\[ p\text{-value} \approx 2 \cdot 0.372 = 0.744. \]

Since \(p\text{-value} \approx 0.74 \gg 0.05\), we fail to reject \(H_0\).

Step 6. Conclusion in context

At the 5% significance level, there is no evidence that the true open rates differ between Subject Line A and Subject Line B. The observed difference (about 28% vs 26.8%) is small compared to the amount of random variation we expect in samples of this size.

These four examples all use the same underlying structure: define parameters and hypotheses about \(p_1 - p_2\), check conditions, compute the pooled proportion and standard error, form a \(z\)-statistic, and then interpret the p-value back in the real-world context.
