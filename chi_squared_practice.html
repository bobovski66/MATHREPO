Problem 1: Expected Counts Under Independence

A survey recorded whether someone owns a pet (X) and whether they live in an apartment (Y) for n = 120 people:

Y = No Y = Yes Total
X = No 18 42 60
X = Yes 30 30 60
Total 48 72 120

(a) Compute all four expected counts under independence.

(b) Which cell has the largest difference O − E?

Solution:

Part (a): Expected Counts

Formula: Eij = (row total × column total) / n

Cell (No pet, No apartment):

E₁₁ = (60 × 48) / 120 = 2880 / 120 = 24

Cell (No pet, Yes apartment):

E₁₂ = (60 × 72) / 120 = 4320 / 120 = 36

Cell (Yes pet, No apartment):

E₂₁ = (60 × 48) / 120 = 2880 / 120 = 24

Cell (Yes pet, Yes apartment):

E₂₂ = (60 × 72) / 120 = 4320 / 120 = 36

Expected counts table:

Y = No Y = Yes Total
X = No 24 36 60
X = Yes 24 36 60
Total 48 72 120

Part (b): Largest Difference

Cell O E O - E
No pet, No apt 18 24 -6
No pet, Yes apt 42 36 +6
Yes pet, No apt 30 24 +6
Yes pet, Yes apt 30 36 -6

Answer: All cells have the same absolute difference of 6. Looking at just positive differences, "No pet, Yes apartment" and "Yes pet, No apartment" both have O - E = +6.

Problem 2: Computing Chi-Squared Statistic

Using the same table from Problem 1:

(a) Compute χ² = Σ (O−E)²/E.

(b) State the degrees of freedom.

(c) Without computing a p-value, explain whether the data appear close to independence or far from independence.

Solution:

Part (a): Chi-Squared Statistic

For each cell, compute (O - E)²/E:

Cell O E O - E (O - E)² (O - E)²/E
No pet, No apt 18 24 -6 36 1.50
No pet, Yes apt 42 36 +6 36 1.00
Yes pet, No apt 30 24 +6 36 1.50
Yes pet, Yes apt 30 36 -6 36 1.00
χ² = 1.50 + 1.00 + 1.50 + 1.00 = 5.00

Part (b): Degrees of Freedom

For a 2 × 2 table:

df = (r - 1)(c - 1) = (2 - 1)(2 - 1) = 1

Part (c): Interpretation

With df = 1, a χ² value of 5.00 is moderate. For reference:

  • χ² close to 0 would suggest data very consistent with independence
  • χ² around 3.84 corresponds to p ≈ 0.05 for df = 1
  • Our χ² = 5.00 is above this threshold

Conclusion: The data appear to deviate somewhat from independence. The p-value would be less than 0.05, suggesting statistically significant evidence of association.

Problem 3: Independence Check and Chi-Squared Test

A college recorded whether students commute by car (X) and whether they arrived late (Y) for n = 200 students:

Not late Late Total
No car 78 22 100
Car 92 8 100
Total 170 30 200

(a) Compute P(Late).

(b) Compute P(Late | Car) and P(Late | No car).

(c) Compute the expected counts under independence.

(d) Compute χ² and identify the cell with the largest contribution.

Solution:

Part (a): Marginal Probability

P(Late) = 30/200 = 0.15

Part (b): Conditional Probabilities

P(Late | No car) = 22/100 = 0.22
P(Late | Car) = 8/100 = 0.08
Note: The conditional probabilities differ from the marginal probability, suggesting the variables are not independent. Students with cars are less likely to be late (8%) compared to the overall rate (15%) and especially compared to students without cars (22%).

Part (c): Expected Counts

Cell Calculation Expected Count
No car, Not late (100 × 170) / 200 85
No car, Late (100 × 30) / 200 15
Car, Not late (100 × 170) / 200 85
Car, Late (100 × 30) / 200 15

Part (d): Chi-Squared Computation

Cell O E O - E (O - E)² (O - E)²/E
No car, Not late 78 85 -7 49 0.576
No car, Late 22 15 +7 49 3.267
Car, Not late 92 85 +7 49 0.576
Car, Late 8 15 -7 49 3.267
χ² = 0.576 + 3.267 + 0.576 + 3.267 ≈ 7.69

Largest contribution: The cells "No car, Late" (3.267) and "Car, Late" (3.267) have the largest contributions.

Interpretation: Students without cars are late more often than independence would predict (O = 22, E = 15), while students with cars are late less often than independence would predict (O = 8, E = 15). This suggests having a car is associated with lower lateness rates.

Statistical conclusion: With df = 1, χ² = 7.69 corresponds to p ≈ 0.0055, providing strong evidence that commute method and lateness are associated (not independent).

Problem 4: Complete Chi-Squared Test

A study recorded whether students attended office hours (X) and whether they earned a B or higher (Y) for n = 50 students:

Grade < B Grade ≥ B Total
No office hours 18 12 30
Office hours 6 14 20
Total 24 26 50

(a) Check the conditions for a chi-squared test.

(b) Compute expected counts and the chi-squared statistic.

(c) State the degrees of freedom and interpret the result.

Solution:

Part (a): Check Conditions

  1. Random sample: Assuming students represent a random or representative sample ✓
  2. Independent observations: Each student appears once ✓
  3. Expected counts: Need to verify all E ≥ 1 and most E ≥ 5 (will check next)

Part (b): Expected Counts and Chi-Squared

Expected counts:

Cell Calculation E
No OH, Grade < B (30 × 24) / 50 14.4
No OH, Grade ≥ B (30 × 26) / 50 15.6
Yes OH, Grade < B (20 × 24) / 50 9.6
Yes OH, Grade ≥ B (20 × 26) / 50 10.4
All expected counts are ≥ 5, so the chi-squared test is appropriate ✓

Chi-squared calculation:

Cell O E (O - E)²/E
No OH, < B 18 14.4 (3.6)²/14.4 = 0.900
No OH, ≥ B 12 15.6 (-3.6)²/15.6 = 0.831
Yes OH, < B 6 9.6 (-3.6)²/9.6 = 1.350
Yes OH, ≥ B 14 10.4 (3.6)²/10.4 = 1.246
χ² = 0.900 + 0.831 + 1.350 + 1.246 ≈ 4.33

Part (c): Degrees of Freedom and Interpretation

df = (2 - 1)(2 - 1) = 1

Hypotheses:

  • H₀: Office hour attendance and grade are independent
  • Hₐ: Office hour attendance and grade are associated

Result: With df = 1 and χ² ≈ 4.33, the p-value ≈ 0.037

Conclusion: Since p < 0.05, we reject H₀. There is statistically significant evidence that attending office hours and earning a B or higher are associated. Looking at the table, students who attended office hours had higher rates of earning B or better (14/20 = 70%) compared to those who didn't attend (12/30 = 40%).
