Confidence Interval Problems

Problem 1 (CI for a proportion)

At a community college, a random sample of \(n = 150\) students is asked whether they work at a paid job during the semester. In the sample, \(93\) students say “yes.”

  1. Compute the sample proportion \(\hat{p}\) of students who work.
  2. Construct a \(95\%\) confidence interval for the true proportion \(p\) of all students at this college who work during the semester.
  3. Interpret your interval in a single sentence in the context of the problem.
Show solution

Step 1: Sample proportion

\[ \hat{p} = \frac{\text{number of “yes”}}{n} = \frac{93}{150} = 0.62. \] So, \(62\%\) of the sample works during the semester.

Step 2: Check conditions

  • Random sample: stated in the problem.
  • Large enough sample: we want \(n\hat{p} \ge 10\) and \(n(1-\hat{p}) \ge 10\). \[ n\hat{p} = 150(0.62) = 93 \ge 10,\quad n(1-\hat{p}) = 150(0.38) = 57 \ge 10. \] Conditions are satisfied.

Step 3: Compute the CI

Use the formula for a \(95\%\) CI for a population proportion: \[ \hat{p} \pm z^\ast \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}, \] where \(z^\ast \approx 1.96\) for \(95\%\) confidence.

First find the standard error: \[ \text{SE} = \sqrt{\frac{0.62(1-0.62)}{150}} = \sqrt{\frac{0.62 \cdot 0.38}{150}} \approx \sqrt{\frac{0.2356}{150}} \approx \sqrt{0.00157} \approx 0.0396. \]

Margin of error: \[ \text{ME} = z^\ast \cdot \text{SE} = 1.96 \cdot 0.0396 \approx 0.0776. \]

CI: \[ 0.62 \pm 0.0776 = (0.5424,\; 0.6976). \] Rounded: \[ (0.54,\; 0.70). \]

Step 4: Interpretation

We are \(95\%\) confident that the true proportion of all students at this community college who work at a paid job during the semester is between \(54\%\) and \(70\%\).

Problem 2 (CI for a mean)

A statistics instructor wants to estimate the average amount of time per week her students spend on this course (including class, homework, and studying). She takes a random sample of \(n = 25\) students and finds:

  • Sample mean: \(\bar{x} = 6.4\) hours
  • Sample standard deviation: \(s = 2.1\) hours

Assume the conditions for using a \(t\)-interval are met.

  1. Construct a \(95\%\) confidence interval for the true mean number of hours students spend on the course per week.
  2. State the interval in a complete sentence.
Show solution

Step 1: Identify what we are estimating

We want a CI for the population mean \(\mu\): the true average weekly time students spend on the course.

Step 2: Use the one-sample \(t\) CI formula

The \(95\%\) CI for \(\mu\) is \[ \bar{x} \pm t^\ast \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}, \] where \(t^\ast\) comes from a \(t\)-distribution with \(n-1 = 24\) degrees of freedom.

For \(95\%\) confidence and \(24\) df, \(t^\ast \approx 2.064\) (from a table or technology).

Step 3: Compute the standard error and margin of error

\[ \text{SE} = \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{2.1}{\sqrt{25}} = \frac{2.1}{5} = 0.42. \]

\[ \text{ME} = t^\ast \cdot \text{SE} = 2.064 \cdot 0.42 \approx 0.867. \]

Step 4: Construct the interval

\[ \bar{x} \pm \text{ME} = 6.4 \pm 0.867 = (6.4 - 0.867,\; 6.4 + 0.867) = (5.533,\; 7.267). \] Rounded, about \((5.53,\; 7.27)\) hours.

Step 5: Interpretation

We are \(95\%\) confident that the true average time students at this college spend on the course per week is between about \(5.5\) and \(7.3\) hours.

Problem 3 (CI & comparing to a claim)

A survey of \(n = 80\) adults in a city finds that their average daily screen time (phone, computer, TV) is \(\bar{x} = 5.1\) hours, with a sample standard deviation of \(s = 1.8\) hours. A local health group claims the mean screen time in the city is \(\mu = 4.5\) hours.

  1. Construct a \(95\%\) confidence interval for the true mean screen time \(\mu\). Assume conditions for a \(t\)-interval are met.
  2. Based on your interval, does the data support or contradict the health group’s claim of \(\mu = 4.5\) hours? Explain briefly.
Show solution

Step 1: CI for the mean

Use \[ \bar{x} \pm t^\ast \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}, \] with \(\bar{x} = 5.1\), \(s = 1.8\), \(n = 80\).

Degrees of freedom: \(n-1 = 79\). For a \(95\%\) CI, \(t^\ast \approx 1.99\) (very close to the normal value \(1.96\)).

Standard error: \[ \text{SE} = \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{1.8}{\sqrt{80}} \approx \frac{1.8}{8.944} \approx 0.201. \]

Margin of error: \[ \text{ME} = t^\ast \cdot \text{SE} \approx 1.99 \cdot 0.201 \approx 0.40. \]

CI: \[ 5.1 \pm 0.40 = (4.70,\; 5.50). \]

Step 2: Compare with the claim \(\mu = 4.5\)

The claimed value \(\mu = 4.5\) hours is not inside the \(95\%\) confidence interval \((4.70,\; 5.50)\).

This suggests that \(4.5\) hours is not a plausible value for the true mean based on this sample. The data contradict the health group’s claim at the \(95\%\) confidence level.

Hypothesis Testing Problems

Problem 4 (test for a proportion)

At a college, a long-standing belief is that about \(40\%\) of students commute from more than 20 miles away. A new random sample of \(n = 120\) students shows that \(60\) of them commute more than 20 miles.

Test, at the \(\alpha = 0.05\) significance level, whether the proportion of students who commute more than 20 miles has changed from \(0.40\).

  1. State the null and alternative hypotheses.
  2. Compute the test statistic \(z\).
  3. Find (or approximate) the p-value.
  4. State your conclusion in context.
Show solution

Step 1: Hypotheses

Let \(p\) be the true proportion of all students who commute more than 20 miles.

\[ H_0: p = 0.40 \] \[ H_a: p \neq 0.40 \] (two-sided test, because we are checking whether it “has changed.”)

Step 2: Sample proportion and conditions

\[ \hat{p} = \frac{60}{120} = 0.50. \]

Check counts under \(H_0\): \[ n p_0 = 120(0.40) = 48 \ge 10, \quad n(1-p_0) = 120(0.60) = 72 \ge 10. \] Normal approximation is appropriate.

Step 3: Test statistic

Use \[ z = \frac{\hat{p} - p_0}{\sqrt{\dfrac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}}. \]

\[ z = \frac{0.50 - 0.40}{\sqrt{\dfrac{0.40(0.60)}{120}}} = \frac{0.10}{\sqrt{\dfrac{0.24}{120}}} = \frac{0.10}{\sqrt{0.002}} = \frac{0.10}{0.04472} \approx 2.24. \]

Step 4: P-value

This is a two-sided test, so \[ p\text{-value} = 2P(Z \ge |2.24|). \] Looking up \(z = 2.24\) (or using technology) gives \(P(Z \ge 2.24) \approx 0.0125\).

So \[ p\text{-value} \approx 2(0.0125) = 0.025. \]

Step 5: Decision and conclusion

Compare the p-value to \(\alpha = 0.05\): \[ 0.025 < 0.05 \Rightarrow \text{reject } H_0. \]

There is statistically significant evidence at the \(5\%\) level that the true proportion of students who commute more than 20 miles is no longer \(0.40\). In this sample, the estimate is higher (\(0.50\)).

Problem 5 (test for a mean, one-sided)

A fitness center advertises that members walk an average of at least \(10{,}000\) steps per day. A skeptical member collects a random sample of \(n = 16\) members and records their average daily steps over a week. The sample mean is \(\bar{x} = 9{,}200\) steps, with sample standard deviation \(s = 1{,}600\) steps.

Assume it is reasonable to use a \(t\)-test. Test, at \(\alpha = 0.05\), whether the data provide evidence that the true mean number of steps is less than \(10{,}000\) steps per day.

  1. State \(H_0\) and \(H_a\).
  2. Compute the test statistic.
  3. Approximate the p-value.
  4. State your conclusion in context.
Show solution

Step 1: Hypotheses

Let \(\mu\) be the true mean number of steps per day for members.

The claim “at least \(10{,}000\)” means the null is \(\mu = 10{,}000\) and we test for less: \[ H_0: \mu = 10{,}000 \] \[ H_a: \mu < 10{,}000. \]

Step 2: Test statistic

Use the one-sample \(t\)-test: \[ t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s / \sqrt{n}} = \frac{9{,}200 - 10{,}000}{1{,}600 / \sqrt{16}}. \]

\[ s / \sqrt{n} = \frac{1{,}600}{4} = 400. \] So \[ t = \frac{-800}{400} = -2.0. \]

Degrees of freedom: \(n - 1 = 15\).

Step 3: P-value

We need \(P(T_{15} \le -2.0)\). Using a \(t\)-table or technology, \(P(T_{15} \le -2.0)\) is around \(0.03\) (exact value depends on table).

So the p-value is approximately \(0.03\).

Step 4: Decision and conclusion

Compare to \(\alpha = 0.05\): \[ 0.03 < 0.05 \Rightarrow \text{reject } H_0. \]

There is statistically significant evidence at the \(5\%\) level that the true mean number of steps per day for members is less than \(10{,}000\). This calls the advertising claim into question.

Problem 6 (test for a proportion, one-sided, and CI connection)

A tutoring center believes that more than \(60\%\) of students who attend at least four tutoring sessions in a term pass their math class. In a sample of \(n = 90\) such students, \(62\) pass the class.

Test the claim at the \(\alpha = 0.05\) level.

  1. State \(H_0\) and \(H_a\).
  2. Compute the test statistic and p-value.
  3. State your conclusion in context.
  4. (Conceptual) If you built a one-sided \(95\%\) lower confidence bound for \(p\), would it be above or below \(0.60\)? Explain briefly using your test result.
Show solution

Step 1: Hypotheses

Let \(p\) be the true proportion of students who pass after at least four tutoring sessions.

“More than 60%” suggests: \[ H_0: p = 0.60 \] \[ H_a: p > 0.60. \]

Step 2: Sample proportion and test statistic

\[ \hat{p} = \frac{62}{90} \approx 0.689. \]

Check counts under \(H_0\): \[ n p_0 = 90(0.60) = 54 \ge 10,\quad n(1-p_0) = 90(0.40) = 36 \ge 10. \] Normal approximation is fine.

Test statistic: \[ z = \frac{\hat{p} - p_0}{\sqrt{\dfrac{p_0(1-p_0)}{n}}} = \frac{0.689 - 0.60}{\sqrt{\dfrac{0.60 \cdot 0.40}{90}}}. \]

Denominator: \[ \sqrt{\dfrac{0.24}{90}} = \sqrt{0.002666\ldots} \approx 0.0516. \]

So \[ z \approx \frac{0.089}{0.0516} \approx 1.72. \]

Step 3: P-value

Since \(H_a: p > 0.60\), this is a right-tailed test: \[ p\text{-value} = P(Z \ge 1.72). \] From a normal table or technology, \(P(Z \ge 1.72) \approx 0.043\).

Step 4: Decision and conclusion

Compare to \(\alpha = 0.05\): \[ 0.043 < 0.05 \Rightarrow \text{reject } H_0. \]

There is statistically significant evidence at the \(5\%\) level that more than \(60\%\) of students who attend at least four tutoring sessions pass their math class.

Step 5: CI connection (one-sided)

A one-sided \(95\%\) lower confidence bound for \(p\) would look like \[ p > \hat{p} - z^\ast \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}} \] with an appropriate \(z^\ast\) for a one-sided \(95\%\) bound.

Since our test at \(\alpha = 0.05\) rejected \(H_0: p = 0.60\) in favor of \(p > 0.60\), the one-sided lower bound should be above \(0.60\). In other words, the CI perspective and the hypothesis test are consistent: both suggest that \(p\) is greater than \(0.60\).
