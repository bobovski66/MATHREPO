Guided Practice: Binomial and Geometric Distributions

In this lesson, we will go through two example problems, each related to a different probability distribution. We’ll determine whether each example involves a binomial or geometric distribution and work through the solution step-by-step.

Example 4.12

Problem: Approximately 70% of statistics students do their homework in time for it to be collected and graded. Each student does homework independently. In a statistics class of 50 students, what is the probability that at least 40 will do their homework on time? Students are selected randomly.

Identifying the Distribution

This problem involves a binomial distribution because:

  • We have a fixed number of trials (50 students).
  • Each trial has two outcomes: a student either completes homework on time or doesn’t.
  • The probability of success (doing homework on time) is constant at 70% or 0.7 for each student.

Part 1: Calculating \( P(X = 40) \)

Let’s calculate the probability that exactly 40 students do their homework on time.

  • \( n = 50 \): The number of students (trials).
  • \( p = 0.7 \): The probability that a student does homework on time.
  • We want \( P(X = 40) \).

Solution: Manual Calculation of \( P(X = 40) \)

The probability of exactly \( k \) successes in a binomial distribution is given by:

\[ P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1 - p)^{n - k} \]

For this problem:

  • \( n = 50 \)
  • \( k = 40 \)
  • \( p = 0.7 \)

Step 1: Calculate the Binomial Coefficient \(\binom{50}{40}\)

\[ \binom{50}{40} = \frac{50!}{40! \cdot (50 - 40)!} = \frac{50!}{40! \cdot 10!} \]

Using a calculator or factorial table, you’ll find:

\[ \binom{50}{40} \approx 10,272,278 \]

Step 2: Calculate \( p^{40} \)

\[ p^{40} = (0.7)^{40} \approx 3.23 \times 10^{-7} \]

Step 3: Calculate \( (1 - p)^{10} \)

\[ (1 - p)^{10} = (0.3)^{10} \approx 5.9 \times 10^{-6} \]

Step 4: Combine the Results

Now, multiply these three components together:

\[ P(X = 40) = \binom{50}{40} \cdot (0.7)^{40} \cdot (0.3)^{10} \] \[ P(X = 40) \approx 10,272,278 \cdot 3.23 \times 10^{-7} \cdot 5.9 \times 10^{-6} \approx 0.0196 \]

Answer: The probability that exactly 40 students do their homework on time is approximately 0.0196 or 1.96%.

Part 2: Calculating \( P(X \geq 40) \)

Now, let’s calculate the probability that at least 40 students do their homework on time, \( P(X \geq 40) \). This is the sum of the probabilities for \( X = 40, 41, \dots, 50 \).

Solution Using Calculator

To calculate \( P(X \geq 40) \), we can use the Online Binomial Calculator:

  1. Set n (number of trials) to 50.
  2. Set p (probability of success) to 0.7.
  3. Select Above and set it to 40 to calculate \( P(X \geq 40) \).
  4. Click "Recalculate" to find the probability.

The calculator gives \( P(X \geq 40) \approx 0.0402 \), or 4.02%.

Example 4.19

Problem: Suppose that you are looking for a student at your college who lives within five miles of you. You know that 55% of the 25,000 students do live within five miles of you. You randomly contact students from the college until one says he or she lives within five miles of you. What is the probability that you need to contact four people?

Identifying the Distribution

This problem involves a geometric distribution because:

  • We are counting the number of trials (contacts) until the first success.
  • Each contact is independent, with a constant probability of success (finding a student who lives within five miles) at 55% or 0.55.

Setting Up the Geometric Distribution

Let’s define the parameters:

  • \( p = 0.55 \): The probability that a student lives within five miles.
  • We want to find the probability that the first success (a student who lives within five miles) occurs on the 4th contact.

Solution

The probability of getting the first success on the 4th trial in a geometric distribution is given by:

\[ P(X = 4) = (1 - p)^{4 - 1} \cdot p \]

Substitute \( p = 0.55 \):

\[ P(X = 4) = (1 - 0.55)^{3} \cdot 0.55 \] \[ = (0.45)^3 \cdot 0.55 \approx 0.050 \]

Answer: The probability that you need to contact four people is approximately 0.050 or 5.0%.

Summary

In these examples, we identified two types of probability distributions:

  • Binomial Distribution (Example 4.12): Used when there’s a fixed number of trials and we’re counting the number of successes.
  • Geometric Distribution (Example 4.19): Used when we’re counting the number of trials until the first success.

Understanding the characteristics of each distribution helps us set up and solve these types of problems effectively!
