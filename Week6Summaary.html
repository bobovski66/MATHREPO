Statistics and Probability Essentials

Welcome! Here’s a quick guide to essential concepts in probability and statistics, with examples to illustrate each one.

1. Random Variable

  • Definition: A random variable is a numerical outcome of a random event. It can be discrete (specific values) or continuous (any value within a range).
  • Example: The number of heads obtained when flipping a coin 10 times is a discrete random variable.

2. Probability Distribution

  • Definition: A probability distribution lists all possible values of a random variable and the probabilities associated with each value. For discrete variables, these probabilities must sum to 1.
  • Example: For a fair six-sided die, the probability distribution assigns a probability of 1/6 to each outcome (1 through 6).

3. Mean (Expected Value)

  • Definition: The mean or expected value (\(\mu\)) of a random variable is the long-term average of its outcomes.
  • Formula: For a random variable with possible values \( x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n \) and probabilities \( p(x_1), p(x_2), \dots, p(x_n) \), the mean is calculated by multiplying each value \( x_i \) by its probability \( p(x_i) \) and adding them all up. This is written in summation notation as: \[ \mu = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i \cdot p(x_i) \] Here, the symbol \(\sum\) (Sigma) means to "sum up" or "add together" each product \( x_i \cdot p(x_i) \) for all \( i \) values from 1 to \( n \).
  • Example: If you roll a fair six-sided die, the expected value of the outcome is 3.5, as calculated by summing each outcome times its probability.

4. Variance and Standard Deviation

  • Variance (\(\sigma^2\)): Measures the spread of outcomes around the mean.
  • Formula: For a random variable with values \( x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n \), mean \(\mu\), and probabilities \( p(x_1), p(x_2), \dots, p(x_n) \), the variance is calculated by taking the difference between each value \( x_i \) and the mean \(\mu\), squaring it, multiplying by \( p(x_i) \), and summing these results. In summation notation: \[ \sigma^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \mu)^2 \cdot p(x_i) \] where \(\sum\) means to add up each result for all \( i \) values from 1 to \( n \).
  • Standard Deviation (\(\sigma\)): The square root of the variance, providing a measure of how much outcomes vary around the mean in the original units of the random variable.
  • Formula: \[ \sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2} \]
  • Example: For a fair six-sided die, the standard deviation of the outcome values is approximately 1.71, indicating the typical deviation from the mean of 3.5.

5. Key Distributions: Binomial and Geometric

Binomial Distribution

  • Definition: The binomial distribution models the number of successes in a fixed number of independent trials, where each trial has only two possible outcomes (e.g., success/failure) and a constant probability of success.
  • Parameters: Number of trials (\( n \)) and probability of success in each trial (\( p \)).
  • Probability of exactly \( k \) successes: \[ P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1 - p)^{n - k} \] where \(\binom{n}{k}\) is the number of ways to choose \( k \) successes from \( n \) trials.
  • Mean (\(\mu\)): \[ \mu = n \cdot p \]
  • Standard Deviation (\(\sigma\)): \[ \sigma = \sqrt{n \cdot p \cdot (1 - p)} \]
  • Example: If you flip a coin 10 times, the binomial distribution can model the probability of getting exactly 5 heads (with \( n = 10 \) and \( p = 0.5 \)).

Geometric Distribution

  • Definition: The geometric distribution models the number of trials needed to achieve the first success in a series of independent trials, each with a constant probability of success.
  • Parameter: Probability of success in each trial (\( p \)).
  • Probability of first success on the \( k \)th trial: \[ P(X = k) = (1 - p)^{k - 1} p \]
  • Mean (\(\mu\)): \[ \mu = \frac{1}{p} \]
  • Standard Deviation (\(\sigma\)): \[ \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1 - p}{p^2}} \]
  • Example: If you’re rolling a die until you get a 6, the geometric distribution can model the probability that you get your first 6 on the 3rd roll (with \( p = \frac{1}{6} \)).

6. Additional Key Terms

  • Outcome: A possible result of a random event.
  • Event: A set of outcomes to which a probability is assigned.
  • Independent Events: Two events are independent if the occurrence of one does not affect the probability of the other.
  • Probability: A number between 0 and 1 representing the likelihood of an event. A probability of 0 means the event will not occur, while a probability of 1 means it will occur with certainty.

Each concept here is a fundamental building block for understanding probability and statistics. Feel free to refer back to this page anytime for a quick refresher!
